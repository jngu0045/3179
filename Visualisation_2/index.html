A Look into Endangered Species

Endangered species can be defined as any species at risk of extinction due to a fast and sudden decrease in its population or loss of habitat. There are various factors that could lead a species into becoming endangered. Australia is home to many unique and diverse species as a result of Australia's geographical isolation. However, more than 480 native animals are at risk of becoming extinct.

This visualisation aims to provide insights on the current status and threats to endangered species.

What is the Red List Index?

The Red List Index show us the trend in overall extinction risk for species. It is based on genuine changes in the number of species in each category of extinction risk on The IUCN Red List of Threatened Species and is expressed as an index ranging from 0 to 1.

The Red List Index value ranges from 1, where all species are categorized as ‘Least Concern', to 0, where all species are categorized as ‘Extinct', thus, indicates how far the set of species has moved overall towards extinction.

On the right, we can see that, New Zealand has one of the lowest Red List Indexes in the world, with a large change between 2001 and 2021.

IUCN Categories

The International Union for Conservation of Nature’s (IUCN) Red List is an indicator of biodiversity at a global level.

The catergories include:

  • “Not Evaluated” (NE)
  • “Data Deficient” (DD)
  • “Least Concern” (LC)
  • “Near Threatened” (NT)
  • “Vulnerable” (VU)
  • “Endangered" (EN)
  • “Critically Endangered" (CR)
  • “Extinct in the Wild” (EW)
  • “Extinct” (EX)

From the heatmap, it can be seen that plants have the highest number of species classified as endangered. 12350 plant species are classified as endangered, 1050 are classified as vulnerable and 427 as critical.

What is Threatening our Species?

Broad and Sub Category Threats

From the stacked bar chart, we can see that plants experience the highest number of threats, followed by birds and mammals. The most predominant threats include habitat loss, fragmentation and disease and invasive species and diseases.

Taking a further look at the sub category threats, we find that invasive weeds are the number one threat, followed by agriculture and aquaculture.

Protected Areas in Australia

This line graph displays how protected areas have changed over time in Australia, from 1950 - 2021.

Protected Areas are determined by IUCN management objectives. The landscape we know today has largely changed as a result of human activities such as food production and forestry. Consequently, we see habitat loss and loss of biodiversity. To prevent further loss, protected areas act to safeguard nature and other cultural resources.

Looking at both marine and terrestrial protected areas, we can see that both have increased largely over time, with the percentage of marine protected areas drastically increasing in 2017. Currently, 20.34% of total terrestrial land is protected in Australia and 40.97% of marine land.
